5 Critical Concepts Project

An interactive essay exploring gender and identity

Introduction

Gender is a widely debated, discussed, and explored topic and for many feels personal and internal. In Western societies, especially, gender is often presented as something we are innately. These gender expectations are thrown onto us early and make it hard to distinguish what is natural or simply Western norms.

This website aims to use interactive features to inspire reflection, education, and conversation surrounding gender. Rather than treating gender as a fixed identity, we will explore how it’s constructed, reinforced, and challenged.

My thesis will tackle how gender is produced by Western cultural narratives, scientific practices, and restrictive fears around how gender should be understood, discussed, and performed. We will focus on 5 concepts to strike a conversation on gender. To begin, the first concept is biological determinism , which is the way Western society presents gender as 2 stable, innate categories determined by biology. Then, we will discuss prescientific anthropomorphism , which touches on how science feels like absolute truth but is still influenced by unconscious bias. The third concept is gender as a performance, which introduces how gender is something we can do and practice. Then, we explore gender across cultures to disprove a universal biological determinism. Finally, we explore anxieties around gender and how disrupting ideas surrounding gender sometimes produces fear. These concepts draw on thinkers like Simone de Beauvoir, María Lugones, Judith Butler, Bernard Saladin D'Anglure, Don Kulick, Anne Fausto-Sterling, and Robin Dembroff who we will explore in detail.

These concepts are critical to the understanding of gender as they will help us understand how gender was constructed and should be discussed moving forward. Once gender is understood as constructed and regulated rather than innate and inevitable, it becomes possible to see both its constraints and its possibilities!!

Concept 1: Biological Determinism

This section discusses biological determinism to show how gender can be framed as something inevitable and innate. Biological determinism is the idea that gender is rooted in biology and thus something fixed and natural. In Western society, this belief can appear neutral but disguises the power structures it reinforces by presenting inequalities as the way things are and should be.

In The Second Sex, Simone de Beauvoir challenges this view by stating the difference between essence and situation (de Beauvoir 1949, 6). Beauvoir states that rather than being born with a fixed gender identity or essence, gender is shaped through lived experience and social conditions, or one’s situation. This separation in ideas helps us understand how something that seems like biological truth is greatly influenced by culture. In a similar way, in Heterosexualism and the Colonial/Modern Gender System, María Lugones discusses how Western colonial systems impose rigid binaries onto Indigenous communities to make them assimilate into patriarchal norms (Lugones 2007, 188). This shows that gender that appears natural is often historically produced and enforced. When we investigate work from those such as Robin Dembroff’s Why Be Nonbinary, this further complicates and challenges biological determinism by showing that gender categories are not exhaustive or fixed (Dembroff 2018). Dembroff discusses how nonbinary identities reveal that gender categories fail to capture the full range of lived experiences, emphasizing that gender should be understood as open-ended rather than strictly defined.

These perspectives reveal that biological determinism doesn’t describe gender but rather constructs ideas that align with Western cultural, historical, and political interests.

Concept 2: Prescientific Antropomorphism

This section expands on biological determinism by showing how science contributes to gender assumptions. Prescientific anthropomorphism defines the tendency humans have to project social assumptions like gender roles onto biology. We often view science as objective, but we need to remember it’s shaped by the cultural contexts in which it was produced.

In Do Sex Hormones Really Exist?, Anne Fausto-Sterling investigates our scientific understanding of sex hormones and how they were influenced by pre-existing ideas about gender (Fausto-Sterling 2000, 177). As an example, hormones were historically labelled as male and female, which reinforced binary thinking even though these hormones were found throughout all bodies and organs at varying levels. These scientific findings influence how we discuss and understand our bodies and relations to them. Since it's impossible to remove these biases from scientific findings completely, we should focus on minimizing them and discussing how our perspectives influence the way we think.

This is an important concept as it shows that science doesn’t entirely uncover the truth about gender and can often mislead and shape our ideas of it. By presenting gendered scientific findings as biological facts, we reinforce the idea that gender is biologically determined and ignore how our biases impact our understanding.

Concept 3: Gender as a Performance

Next, this section introduces gender as something that’s produced through performance and repetition rather than something purely biological. Gender performativity is theorized by Judith Butler in Performative Acts and Gender Constitution and discusses how gender is something we continuously do through repeated actions, behaviours, and norms (Butler 1990, 519). These performances can include how we dress, speak, and behave.

This concept of performativity suggests that gender appears stable because it’s performed consistently over time. The performances are also shaped by social expectations which are rooted in Western norms. These acts are not entirely voluntary as we can be constrained by what’s socially acceptable. This pushes individuals to often perform gender in recognizable ways.

Western cultural narratives regulate how gender can be performed which makes certain expressions seem natural while marginalizing others. By recognizing gender as a performance, we can better analyze how it's regulated and used as a tool in society.

Concept 4: Gender Across Cultures

In this section, we expand the discussion by showing how Western narratives of gender are not universal. Gender across cultures highlights the diverse ways societies can conceptualize and organize genders beyond Western frameworks.

In The ‘Third Gender’ of the Inuit, Bernard Saladin d’Anglure describes how Inuit communities organize genders in ways that do not align with Western binaries, but rather as fluid and relational (Saladin d’Anglure 2006, 135). This is different than the structured, firm ways Western society frames gender. Similarly, Don Kulick, in his work on Brazilian travesties, illustrates how gender identity in Brazil is formed based on sexuality as well as social relations (Kulick 1998, 574). Compared to the Western binary, this challenges the ideas that biology determines gender. In Heterosexualism and the Colonial/Modern Gender System, María Lugones argues that the imposed Western gender systems erase and reshape Indigenous understandings of gender (Lugones 2007, 188). This highlights how Western gender norms are not only culturally specific but also historically enforced. Each of these pieces helps us understand various cultural and social impacts of gender. Additionally, intersectionality shows us that gender can’t be understood in isolation (Crenshaw 1989, 139). It teaches us that our identities intersect and evolve together.

This concept is critical as it disrupts the idea that Western gender categories are universal. Instead, it reveals that gender is one of the systems reinforced and constructed through cultural narratives rather than purely through biology.

Reflection prompt: How does this erasure of how other cultures understand gender impact our own understanding? Try writing a short note or thinking about one concrete example.

Concept 5: Anxieties Around Gender

In the last section, we look at how gender remains a topic that can be difficult to understand, discuss, and question. Anxieties around gender emerge when ideas we have around gender are disrupted. Gender structures relate to our identities, social organizations, and scientific knowledge, so challenging it can feel destabilizing.

In Gender Ideology and the Fear of Destruction, Judith Butler describes how anti-gender movements construct fears around gender as a threat to social order (Butler 2021, 5). Butler refers to this as a phantasmic scene, which means that fears are exaggerated to project an idea about gender theory being dangerous or destructive. These reactions show how gender isn’t neutral but is something that involves emotions and open conversations.

This concept is helpful in understanding the thesis as it explains why the idea of gender being practiced rather than innate is often met with resistance. If gender were truly biologically determined, it would not require constructed fears to defend it. This final concept ties the project together by showing how Western gender narratives are maintained through society, science, and emotional boundaries that restrict how gender can be understood and discussed.

Conclusion

This project was set to challenge our assumptions about gender by exploring how it can be shaped through Western cultural narratives, scientific practices, and anxieties. The 5 concepts of biological determinism, prescientific anthropomorphism, gender as a performance, gender across cultures, and anxieties around gender aimed to show that gender is rich with history, opportunity, culture, and freedom rather than just a set of prescribed Western norms.

This project also opens some questions we did not answer, such as the intersection of gender with race, disability, and class or how gender is treated in societies that do not have as much Western impact compared to places like Canada. Additionally, we critiqued existing and incomplete definitions of gender but did not investigate how we can dismantle these definitions to push for a more gender inclusive society. Some great next steps would include expanding the analysis to explore a broader range of perspectives and thinking deeply about how we can create more gender inclusive communities.

Medium

I chose the medium of an interactive website to present gender as a concept that requires active thinking and reflection rather than just information. The format of toggles, interactive questions, and widgets encourages exploration rather than passive reading.

The intended audience is broad and meant for anyone interested in developing a deeper understanding of gender. I also designed the website with accessibility practices in mind.

Glossary

Anti-Gender Ideology

An ideology that opposes changing or expanding understandings of gender, often framing these changes as threats to social order

Biological Determinism

The idea that gender is rooted in biology and therefore fixed, natural, and inevitable

Essence

Described by Simone de Beauvoir as a fixed, innate identity or nature that one is assumed to be born with

Gender (as framed in the essay)

Not a fixed or innate identity, but something constructed, reinforced, and challenged through cultural narratives, scientific practices, and social expectations

Gender Across Cultures

The idea that different societies conceptualize and organize gender in diverse ways

Gender Binary

The classification of gender into two distinct and fixed categories (male and female)

Gender Performativity

Described by Judith Butler as something we continuously do through repeated actions, behaviors, and norms

Intersectionality

The concept that gender cannot be understood in isolation but is interconnected with other aspects of identity

Nonbinary Identities

Gender identities that fall outside the traditional binary

Phantasmic Scene

Described by Judith Butler as a term to describe exaggerated or imagined fears that portray gender theory as dangerous or destructive

Prescientific Anthropomorphism

The tendency to project human social assumptions (like as gender roles) onto biology

Sex Hormones

Hormones historically labeled as male or female despite existing in all bodies at varying levels

Situation

Described by Simone de Beauvoir as the lived experience and social conditions that shape a person’s identity, including gender

Travesti

A form of gender identity described by Don Kulick where gender is shaped by sexuality and social relations
